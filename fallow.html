Writing Guide
First Drafts
"Get it down, then get it right. The first draft is just you telling yourself the story."
Write forward, not backward
Resist the urge to edit as you go. A finished messy draft is infinitely more useful than a polished half. Fix sentences in revision — you can't revise a blank page.
Silence your inner critic
The critical mind and the creative mind cannot fully occupy the same space. Draft with the door closed. Invite the critic in only once the story exists.
Quantity enables quality
Write more than you need. Scenes, fragments, alternate versions. You will cut most of it — and in doing so, find the book underneath.
Scene Construction
Every scene needs a turn
Something should change within a scene — a relationship, a belief, a power balance. If a character ends a scene in exactly the same position they began it, the scene may not be earning its place.
Enter late, leave early
Begin scenes as close to the significant moment as possible. End them before the natural conclusion. The reader fills the gap — and that act of filling is where they become invested.
Ground in the senses first
Before abstract emotion or reflection, give the reader something physical to inhabit: a smell, a texture, a sound. Sensation is the fastest route into a scene.
The iceberg principle
What a scene withholds matters as much as what it shows. The subtext — what characters don't say, don't do, don't acknowledge — is often where the real meaning lives.
Character
Want and need are different things
A character's want drives the plot. Their need — what they must reckon with internally — drives the arc. The most satisfying stories let these collide.
Show agency, not just suffering
Even a character in an impossible situation makes choices. Showing those choices — however small — is what makes a character feel alive rather than acted upon.
Contradiction is truth
Real people contain opposing impulses. A character who is only one thing will feel thin. Allow your characters to surprise even you.
Secondary characters deserve interiority
Even characters who exist to serve the plot have lives, histories, and opinions that exist off the page. Knowing these — even if they never appear — makes them feel three-dimensional.
Prose & Voice
Trust the reader
State something once and trust it landed. Resist the urge to explain, underline, or repeat. The reader is on your side — let them do some of the work.
Vary sentence rhythm
Short sentences create urgency and emphasis. Longer sentences slow the reader, allow thought to unfold, create a sense of being held inside a moment. The contrast between them is where rhythm lives.
Earn your adjectives
One precise, unexpected modifier does more than three familiar ones. "The cold grey morning" tells us nothing. "The morning that smelled of last night's fire" puts us somewhere specific.
Avoid throat-clearing
Watch for sentences that exist only to set up the next sentence. Cut to the actual thought directly.
Dialogue
People talk past each other
Real conversation is rarely direct. Characters answer different questions than the ones asked, pursue their own agenda, deflect. Dialogue that is too efficient often feels theatrical.
What is not said
The most powerful moments in dialogue are often the silences — what a character cannot bring themselves to say, or says through action rather than words.
Said is invisible
The word "said" disappears when reading. Reach for alternatives only when the manner of speaking is genuinely meaningful.
Structure & Pacing
The three-act spine
Most stories follow: a world disrupted, a struggle through the disruption, and a resolution (however partial). Knowing where your story sits on this spine helps when you lose your footing.
Pace with intention
Slow scenes give weight to fast ones. If everything moves at the same speed, the reader loses their sense of what matters.
End on momentum, not resolution
Chapter endings that resolve completely invite the reader to put the book down. Ending on a question, a tension, or an image that demands following keeps the pages turning.
Revision
Read it aloud
The ear catches what the eye misses. Awkward rhythm, unintentional repetition, sentences that are grammatically correct but impossible to read — these reveal themselves instantly when spoken.
Cut the first paragraph
Most chapters begin one paragraph too early. The real opening is usually the second paragraph. Try it — it works more often than it should.
Kill your darlings, selectively
The question is whether the passage serves the story, not whether you love it. Keep what earns its place.
